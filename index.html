A Modern Overview of Linux Ricing
> Introduction
Linux's customizability is one of the key reasons why I, as well as many other FOSS enthusiasts, daily drive it as our main OS. It's no wonder then, that a large and welcoming community formed around tailoring Linux to become a perfect fit for your workflow and aesthetic preferences. This community, dubbed "ricers" after the similar hobby of customizing cars, has created pieces of software that truly allow anyone of any skill level with Linux to shape their experience to match their tastes. This article aims to be an overview of the modern software options novices should consider when creating their own setup.
> Preperations
Ricing can be annoying, difficult, and devoid of many benefits without the proper preperations. While you can technically make any rice you want from any starting point, certain starting points will be much easier to theme than others. Before we get started, here are some general words of advice:
  • Use either a minimal distro, or a distro that's designed around the window manager/desktop environment you plan on ricing. Barebones distros like EndeavorOS will grant you greater flexibility in customizing your desktop than "batteries included" options like Ubuntu, while an distro like Fedora is known for offering a highly polished GNOME environment out of the box.

  • Get your system usable before customizing it. Installing a web browser and a terminal emulator at the beginning will make the rest of your ricing much easier.

  • Get comfortable navigating your system from the TTY, as it's a reliable tool for fixing broken setups. DistroTube's tutorial serves as a good introduction to the basics of navigating the TTY.

  • Have a USB with a batteries included, live ISO on hand for situations where your rice is broken beyond reasonable repair.
> Parts
Linux's modularity is a key reason as to why it's so customizable. It is thanks to this modularity, that you're able to pick and choose every single aspect of your desktop experience. Here is a list of potential "parts" that are often seen in conventional Linux setups, but you are by no means required to have all, or even any of them in your own rice:
  • Display Servers
    The display protocol is responsible for handling communication between various parts of your system in order to draw applications to the screen. Without one, you could only use the command line. For the longest time, this decision was made for you, as there used to be only one industry standard. In fact, if this guide were made 5-10 years ago, this section wouldn't even exist!
    • X11
      The X11 display protocol has been around since the mid-late 80s. With its age, it carries a key benefit all ancient softwares implicitly have. The vast majority of applications, even now, are primarily designed with X11 support in mind. Unfortunately, this is where the advantages end.

      “X[11] works extremely well for what it is, but what it is is deeply flawed” - Adam Jackson, X.Org Project Owner

      X11 is old, and fundamentally broken in more ways than one. Going into its many issues and the reasons it fell out of the limelight in recent times is out of the scope of this guide. For a more detailed dive into X11's failings, this article by blogger Eric Griffith, and ex-board member of the xorg foundation, Daniel Stone, paints a full picture regarding why the software garnered such a reputation. The current recommendation is to use it only if you aren't able to use the modern alternative, Wayland.

    • Wayland
      Wayland is the hip new kid on the display server block, and is an objective improvement over X11 in almost every single way. Its system wide, triple-buffer v-sync implementation completely eliminates screen tearing across all computers capable of running it, and its minimal overhead dramatically improves application startup to draw times.

      However, trying to replace what has been the standard for nearly 40 years isn't easy. Wayland has issues when running under NVidia GPUs, ranging from it not working at all to artifacting when running specific apps. It is generally recommended that people running NVidia graphics cards stick to X11. Applications that are only native to X11 run under a translation layer dubbed XWayland. This translation layer is generally very good and applications running under it should enjoy near native performance, but it does introduce increased startup times.

    The rest of this guide will include separate options for both X11 and Wayland protocols.

  • Window Managers/Desktop Environments
    One of the key parts of any custom desktop is... The desktop itself of course! When it comes to applications that manage your desktop, you have two options:
    • Window Managers, which are light-weight and minimal pieces of software that simply manage where and how an application window will render and little else. Window managers can be split into two main groups with some supporing both approaches. Floating, and Tiling:
      • Tiling Window Managers automatically re-position all the windows on your screen whenever a new window appears. Some people find this more productive than manually positioning windows themselves, while others dislike the lack of control. Examples of popular tiling window managers are:
        • Wayland
          • Sway(GitHub)
          • Hyprland(GitHub)
          • River(GitHub)
          • Qtile(Main Page)
          • More wayland window managers can be found here
        • X11
          • i3(GitHub)
          • Awesome(Main Page)
          • dwm(Main Page)
          • BSPWM(GitHub)
          • Qtile(Main Page)
          • XMonad(GitHub)

      • Floating Window Managers are similar to the more conventional desktop experience you'd expect out of any modern operating system. When you start an application, it's up to you to position it or toggle maximize/fullscreen for the specific window. Examples of popular floating window managers are:
        • Wayland
          • Wayfire(GitHub)
          • Labwc(GitHub)
        • X11
          • Openbox(GitHub)
          • Awesome(Main Page)
          • 2bwm(GitHub)

    • Desktop Environments, which aim to be complete desktop experiences. They often times come with a window manager built in along with other pieces of software like a taskbars, screenshot tools, terminals, etc. Some examples of Desktop Environments include:
      • Wayland
        • GNOME(Main Page)
        • KDE(Main Page)
      • X11
        • GNOME(Main Page)
        • KDE(Main Page)
        • XFCE(Main Page)
        • LXQT(Main Page)
        • Budgie(GitHub)
        • Mate(Main Page)

  • Status Bars

  • File Browsers
    File Browsers in Linux are diverse in both customizability, as well as feature set. Options range from Thunar, which is a very customizeable and powerful GUI file broswer, to Ranger, a file broswer which runs inside the command line.

    You should be using Firefox(Main Page), as it's currently the only major browser that maintains support for UBlock Origin, which is by far the gold standard for ad blockers.

    Screenshot Tools in Linux range from extremely minimal scripts to full blown GUIs. It's important to note that large images can take some time to copy to the clipboard on X11. A common issue users run into is accidentally pasting the previous contents of their clipboard as linux hasn't finished the transfer yet.

  • Wallpaper Managers
    Wallpaper Managers are usually very simple and straight forward. Most of the time, whichever one your Desktop Environment or Window Manager came with is the best choice. However, in situations where your environment didn't come with one pre-installed, Nitrogen and feh are good options.

  • Brightness Adjusters
    Brightness Adjusters that use a GUI can be tedious for what is otherwise a very simple feature. CLI tools such as brightnessctl or light allow the user to manage their screen brightness via keybinds or their status bar.

> Conclusion
Ricing is not only a way to tailor your desktop around your prefered workflow, it's also a way for Linux enthusiasts to express themselves. It's a way to showcase your tastes, increase your knowledge of the Linux ecosystem, and for many, it's a way to make lifelong friends. The ricing community is one of the most supportive groups you'll ever find, and strives to make the hobby as inclusive and welcoming as possible. Make sure to check out the r/unixporn reddit and discord server if you ever need help or run into any issues during your ricing journey!
